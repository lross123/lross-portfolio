Masters Dissertation

This page provides an overview of my masters dissertation. The work explores mathematical models for tissue growth using stochastic processes, combining theoretical analysis with computational simulation. The project showcases my ability to perform advanced research, derive and implement complex algorithms, and communicate findings to both technical and non‑technical audiences.

Background

Tissue growth is inherently stochastic; individual cell behaviours and micro‑environmental factors introduce randomness at multiple scales. Understanding how these random interactions lead to emergent macroscopic patterns can improve our knowledge of biological development and inform therapeutic strategies.

Objectives

  • Derive stochastic differential equations to model tissue growth and cell proliferation under different biological assumptions.
  • Develop numerical methods to simulate these processes efficiently across spatial domains and parameter regimes.
  • Analyse the statistical properties of simulated tissue growth and compare them with experimental or published data.
  • Explore potential applications of the models in medicine and biotechnology.

Key Findings

The dissertation demonstrates that stochastic models capture heterogeneity and randomness more accurately than deterministic approaches. Simulations reveal how small fluctuations can lead to significant variations in growth trajectories. Parameter sweeps highlight critical thresholds where tissue patterns transition from homogeneous to heterogeneous growth.
